Title: The Merry Adventures of Robin Hood


Author: Howard Pyle


Number of pages: 394


Difficulty: 1


Type of Book: Novel


Setting of place: Sherwood Forest, Nottingham Town, Finsbury fields etc.


Setting of atmosphere: Tense, Warm and Crowded


Why i choose this book ?: I choose this book because this book presents an interesting and action-filled adventure story, with the famous character Robin Hood as a folk hero who fights injustice. The narrative combines elements of humor, courage, and justice, making it interesting and entertaining for all groups. Apart from that, this book also provides a view of life in the past with a strong historical background, but conveyed in simple language and rich in imagination. The characters in this story, especially Robin Hood, are developed with interesting and complex traits,full of intelligence, courage, and a sense of justice that make the reader feel close and interested in following their journey. Through stories full of intrigue and conflict, this book invites readers to reflect on the concepts of justice, morality, and resistance to injustice that are still relevant today. In addition to providing entertainment, this book also indirectly brings readers to know the social and cultural background of England in the Middle Ages in a light and enjoyable way. Each chapter in this book often stands alone with different stories, making each part have its own characteristics and maintain the reader's interest from beginning to end. Reading this book also means appreciating classic works that have influenced many modern literatures and widely known adaptations, expanding the understanding of literacy and appreciation for the history of folklore. The book adopts a rich storytelling style with fresh conversations and simple humor, so that the reading experience feels similar to listening to a story from a skilled narrator, who can create a warm and close atmosphere. With evocative explanations and detailed descriptions of situations, the book brings the reader's visual imagination to life, as if they were involved in an adventure in Sherwood Forest and facing the villains.


Characters:
1.Robin Hood - The main character, a brave and intelligent folk hero, leads a group of charitable robbers in Sherwood Forest who fight injustice.

2.Little John - Robin Hood's loyal friend, a big and strong man, an expert in stick fighting.

3.Will Scarlet - An important member of Robin Hood's group who is famous for his bravery and loyalty.

4.Allan a Dale - A young man who joins Robin Hood, known as a skilled singer and archer.

5.Sheriff of Nottingham - Robin Hood's main enemy, a corrupt and cruel official from the city of Nottingham.

6.Queen Eleanor - The queen who is present as a powerful figure and plays a role in several parts of the story.

7.King Henry II - The king who is depicted as an influential and powerful figure in his time.

8.Richard the Lionheart - The prince who later became king, known for his bravery and chivalry, and as an ally of Robin Hood.

9.Bishop of Hereford - A church figure who is often seen wearing a religious robe, sometimes involved in the intrigue of the story.

10.Adam Bell, Clym o' the Clough, William of Cloudesly, Eric o' Lincoln - Other known highwaymen in folklore who appear as supporting characters in the narrative.

11.Will Stutely, John Little (before he changed his name to Little John), Midge the Miller, Arthur a Bland - Members of Robin Hood's band who contribute to the adventures and struggles.

Plot:
The Merry Adventures of Robin Hood narrates the tale of Robin Hood, a valiant outlaw residing in Sherwood Forest alongside his devoted companions. He is celebrated for his bold exploits, particularly his knack for outsmarting the Sheriff of Nottingham and his guards. The narrative unfolds as Robin Hood earns his reputation after being declared an outlaw for illegally hunting deer within the king's preserved lands. He recruits a band of talented outlaws who share his values of fairness and friendship. As the story progresses, Robin Hood and his merry companions partake in courageous and mischievous activities, such as taking from the wealthy to support the needy, confronting corrupt authorities, and aiding those in distress. Important moments include Little John meeting an elderly woman whose children are sentenced for pursuing a deer, Robin Hood’s renowned archery competitions where he displays unmatched prowess, and numerous confrontations and discussions with the Sheriff.


My general opinion: The Merry Adventures of Robin Hood by Howard Pyle is a charming and classic rendition of the famous tales surrounding Robin Hood. This book captures the essence of medieval folklore with an upbeat and engaging style that attracts both children and adults. Pyle’s storytelling weaves together excitement, humor, and romance, making the narratives animated and unforgettable. The characters, particularly Robin Hood and his group of jovial companions, are vividly brought to life with unique traits, enhancing the richness of the stories. The book strongly conveys themes of justice, faithfulness, and defiance against oppression, making it not only enjoyable but also morally uplifting. One of the most admirable features of this book is its ability to intertwine historical fantasy with moral teachings. Robin Hood’s role as a defender of the downtrodden and a foe of wrongdoing is still relevant and motivational today. Moreover, the language and illustrations found in the original editions greatly enhance the reader's experience, giving a glimpse into the charm and atmosphere of that time. Nonetheless, since it is a classic piece, certain elements of the language or stylistic choices may seem outdated to contemporary audiences. However, this does not diminish the overall pleasure and importance of the book. Instead, it offers insight into the development of folklore and narrative art. In summary, "The Merry Adventures of Robin Hood" is recognized as a significant work within the realm of English literature, praised for its lively storytelling and representation of heroic values. The novel remains a seminal work of literature that has influenced numerous adaptations and continues to be an enjoyable introduction to the Robin Hood mythology. Its cultural value, entertainment value, and hidden moral make it a worthwhile read, especially for those interested in classic literature and folklore.

What did i like least ?:

1.Old and somewhat complex style: Since this is an old work, the style uses sentence fragments and vocabulary that may feel a bit heavy or old-fashioned to modern readers, so it requires extra effort to understand it.

2.The plot is sometimes repetitive and long: There are parts of the story with quite long and detailed descriptions that may make the reading rhythm slow, especially for readers who like fast-paced narratives.

3.Characters and plot are sometimes stereotypical: In some parts, the characters and situations are described in a standard pattern (clear good vs. evil), so that it does not provide surprises or deep character complexity.

4.Focus on entertainment and light fantasy: If readers are looking for a completely accurate historical story or a very serious narrative, this book may feel less satisfying because it emphasizes the entertainment and fantasy elements.

5.The impact of overly explicit morals: The book sometimes includes overly explicit and patronizing morals. Some modern readers looking for a story with more subtle nuance or open interpretation may find this approach a bit force.

6.Limited gender representation: As a late 19th-century classic, the female characters in the book tend to be a minority and are often depicted in traditional stereotypes, which can feel less relevant or empowering to modern readers.

7.Differences from the original legend: The version of Robin Hood in this book is a more light-hearted and humorous adaptation than the more serious and sometimes dark version of the original legend. This can be disappointing for readers expecting a more historically faithful tale.

8.Inconsistent use of dialect and writing style: In some passages, the author uses an old dialect or accent that sometimes changes and inconsistent, which can be confusing for the reader and disrupt the flow of reading.

9.Limitations in the development of cultural and social background: The book provides a very simplistic picture of Robin Hood's world, without much detailed explanation of the social, economic, or political conditions underlying the story, so it feels lacking in historical context.
